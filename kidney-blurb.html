Alex Needs A Kidney!

My name is Bennett (Alex) Myers and I have end-stage chronic kidney disease (CKD). In 2013, when I was 21 years old, I discovered that my kidneys were failing with about 20% function remaining. I’ve been on the list to receive a deceased donor kidney for about 3 years now but the prospect of receiving a kidney through the transplant list is still at least 3 or 4 years away. My current kidney function is around 12% and while my condition has generally been stable, my doctors tell me that it is highly likely I will need dialysis if I keep waiting for a deceased donor kidney. I had been holding out hope for my kidneys getting better or for advances in artificial/cloned organ technology but after my most recent transplant evaluation appointment, I realized that it's time to get serious about reaching out to potential living donors. There are many reasons why receiving a living donor kidney is ideal:
  • As mentioned earlier, dialysis is likely if I don't find a living donor. Dialysis, while life-saving, increases risks of heart and bone disease, infection, and a decreased life expectancy, as well as a decreased quality of life.
  • Living donor kidneys tend to last twice as long (around 10 years for a deceased donor kidney, and 15-20 for a living donor kidney). Because of my age, I will need at least one more kidney transplant after this one in my lifetime and so it is important for the transplanted kidney to last as long as possible.
  • With a living donor, it is possible to time the surgery for the optimal health and convenience of both the recipient and the donor.
The main qualification necessary for becoming a living donor is being in good overall physical and mental health. The process involves a thorough medical evaluation which involves blood and urine tests, as well as radiological imaging and psychological evaluation. If it is determined that you are not a match, it is still possible to help by donating a kidney through the Kidney Paired Donation program which matches a donor-recipient pair with another pair whose donor has a compatible kidney. This way, kidney transplant patients with willing but incompatible donors can receive compatible kidneys.
